How does ionic flow complete the circuit? (circled numbers match the canvas overlay)

① Oxidation at the Zn anode

Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻. Each zinc atom that dissolves releases 2 electrons into the wire and leaves a Zn²⁺ ion in solution — making the anode half-cell increasingly positive.

② Electrons flow through the wire

The 2e⁻ travel through the external wire from Zn to Cu — this movement of electrons is the electric current measured by the voltmeter. No ions are involved here.

③ Reduction at the Cu cathode

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu. Electrons arrive and reduce Cu²⁺ ions out of solution — removing positive charge from the cathode half-cell and making it increasingly negative.

④ Salt bridge restores charge balance

K⁺ migrates from the bridge into the cathode — replacing the lost Cu²⁺ charge. NO₃⁻ migrates into the anode — neutralising the excess Zn²⁺ charge. Both half-cells stay electrically neutral.

Key concept: Ions do not carry the same electrons round a loop. They carry charge by physically migrating to erase the charge imbalance that would otherwise create a back-EMF and halt electron flow. Think of it as a charge relay: electrons run the outside lane, ions run the inside lane — together they keep the circuit alive.
