Core Concepts

What is a Salt Bridge?

A salt bridge is typically a piece of filter paper soaked in an inert electrolyte solution (e.g., \( KNO_3(aq) \)), or a U-tube filled with an electrolyte gel. It is used to connect two half-cells in a Galvanic (Voltaic) cell.

The Two Main Functions

  • Completes the Circuit: It allows the flow of mobile ions between the two half-cells, completing the electrical circuit so electrons can flow through the external wire. Without it, the circuit is open, and the voltage drops to zero immediately.
  • Maintains Electrical Neutrality: As the redox reaction proceeds, charge builds up in the half-cells. The salt bridge prevents this buildup by providing counter-ions.

At the Anode (Oxidation)

E.g., Zinc half-cell: $$Zn(s) \rightarrow Zn^{2+}(aq) + 2e^-$$

Positive \( Zn^{2+} \) ions are produced. To prevent excess positive charge buildup, Anions (e.g., \( NO_3^- \)) from the salt bridge flow into the anode half-cell.

At the Cathode (Reduction)

E.g., Copper half-cell: $$Cu^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow Cu(s)$$

Positive \( Cu^{2+} \) ions are consumed, leaving excess negative spectator ions (e.g., \( SO_4^{2-} \)). To neutralize this, Cations (e.g., \( K^+ \)) from the salt bridge flow into the cathode half-cell.

Choosing an Electrolyte

The electrolyte in the salt bridge must be inert. It must not react with the solutions in either half-cell to form a precipitate.

Example: \( KNO_3 \) is highly preferred because \( K^+ \) and \( NO_3^- \) salts are always soluble. \( KCl \) cannot be used if one half-cell contains \( Ag^+ \) or \( Pb^{2+} \) ions, because insoluble \( AgCl(s) \) or \( PbCl_2(s) \) will form, blocking the salt bridge.
